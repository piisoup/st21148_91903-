Black Holes

Imagine something in space that's so heavy and dense that nothing, not even light, can escape its pull. That's a black hole! It's not actually a hole you can fall into like in a cartoon; it's a place where gravity is so strong that it warps the fabric of space and time. This extreme gravity is created when a massive star collapses in on itself at the end of its life, crushing all of its matter into an infinitely small point called a singularity. The "point of no return" around this singularity is called the event horizon. Once something crosses this invisible line, it's trapped forever, destined to be pulled toward the center. Scientists can't see black holes directly because they don't emit any light, but they can detect them by observing the way they affect nearby stars and gas, which often orbit the black hole and get pulled in, heating up and emitting powerful X-rays.

Comets

Imagine something that's basically a giant, cosmic "dirty snowball" made of frozen gases, rock, and dust. That's a comet! They spend most of their lives way out in the deep freeze of the solar system, but every once in a while, something nudges them into a long, looping orbit that brings them closer to the Sun. As a comet gets closer, the Sun's heat causes the ice to turn directly into gas, a process called sublimation. This gas and dust create a massive, glowing cloud around the core, called the coma, and a beautiful, long tail that can stretch for millions of miles. Because of the solar wind and pressure from sunlight, the tail always points away from the Sun, no matter which direction the comet is traveling. Studying comets is like getting a glimpse into a time machine; they're thought to be ancient leftovers from when our solar system was first formed over 4.6 billion years ago.
